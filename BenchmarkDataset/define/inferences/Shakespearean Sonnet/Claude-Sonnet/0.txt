A Shakespearean Sonnet (also known as an English Sonnet) is a 14-line poem with a specific rhyme scheme and structure. Here are its key characteristics:

1. Rhyme Scheme: ABAB CDCD EFEF GG
- Three quatrains (four-line stanzas)
- One couplet (two-line stanza)

2. Meter: Written in iambic pentameter
- Each line has 10 syllables
- Follows a pattern of unstressed/stressed syllables

3. Structure:
- First quatrain: Introduces the theme/problem
- Second quatrain: Develops the theme
- Third quatrain: Introduces a twist or deepens the theme
- Final couplet: Provides resolution or summary

4. Common themes:
- Love
- Beauty
- Time
- Mortality
- Nature

Shakespeare wrote 154 sonnets using this form, which is why it bears his name. Some famous examples include "Sonnet 18" ("Shall I compare thee to a summer's day?") and "Sonnet 130" ("My mistress' eyes are nothing like the sun").
